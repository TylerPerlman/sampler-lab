# Public References and Provenance

Sampler Lab is authored by Tyler Perlman with AI-assisted implementation and documentation.
Mathematical claims, generated code, and examples are reviewed through deterministic identities,
strict typing, executable documentation, invariance checks, fixed-seed statistical tests, and
cross-method reference experiments. The project does not include private source documents, copied
course assignments, copied prompts, or third-party diagrams.

The following references provide public provenance for major algorithm families:

- Metropolis, Rosenbluth, Rosenbluth, Teller, and Teller (1953), *Equation of State Calculations by Fast Computing Machines*.
- Hastings (1970), *Monte Carlo Sampling Methods Using Markov Chains and Their Applications*.
- Geman and Geman (1984), *Stochastic Relaxation, Gibbs Distributions, and the Bayesian Restoration of Images*.
- Gelfand and Smith (1990), *Sampling-Based Approaches to Calculating Marginal Densities*.
- Neal (2001), *Annealed Importance Sampling*.
- Del Moral (2004), *Feynman-Kac Formulae*.
- Robert and Casella (2004), *Monte Carlo Statistical Methods*.
- Neal (2011), *MCMC Using Hamiltonian Dynamics*.
- Goodman and Weare (2010), *Ensemble Samplers with Affine Invariance*.
- Andrieu and Thoms (2008), *A Tutorial on Adaptive MCMC*.
- Roberts and Rosenthal (2009), *Examples of Adaptive MCMC*.
- Liu and Wang (2016), *Stein Variational Gradient Descent*.
- Botev, L'Ecuyer, and Tuffin (2013), *Markov Chain Importance Sampling with Applications to Rare Event Probability Estimation*.

Method pages state package-specific sign, scaling, and normalization choices explicitly. Where two
valid conventions exist, the implementation documents the selected convention rather than relying
on an external source's notation.
